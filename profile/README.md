# How to Get Through the First Year of Marriage (First Year of Marriage Fighting All the Time)

The first year of marriage is supposed to feel like the best chapter of your life. You just made a lifelong promise to someone you love. But if you are reading this right now, chances are it does not feel that magical. It feels more like a battleground. Arguments that come out of nowhere. Tension that follows you to bed. That quiet, unsettling question in the back of your mind: "Did we make a mistake?" I want you to hear this clearly: you did not make a mistake. You are simply going through one of the hardest and most underrated transitions that two people can make together, and nobody warned you how rough it could be.

I often hear from couples who come to me six or eight months into marriage, completely blindsided. They say things like, "We never fought like this when we were dating." And that is exactly the point. Marriage is not an extension of dating. It is a completely different thing. The roles shift. The stakes feel higher. The habits you could overlook before suddenly feel impossible to ignore. Fighting in the first year is not a sign that your marriage is broken. It is a sign that two separate people with separate histories are learning to share one life. That process is hard. But it is workable.

**Understanding Why the First Year Is So Hard**

Before you can fix a problem, you need to understand what is actually causing it. Most couples walk into marriage thinking love is enough to carry them through. And love matters enormously. But love alone does not teach you how to split household responsibilities without resentment. It does not tell you whose family to spend the holidays with. It does not show you how to handle money when you have different spending habits. These are the real issues hiding beneath the surface of first-year fights.

What is actually happening in most cases is a collision of two very different sets of expectations. You both grew up in different homes, with different rules, different ways of handling conflict, different definitions of what a "good spouse" looks like. You carry all of that into the marriage without even realizing it. So when your partner does something that seems perfectly normal to them, it can feel deeply wrong to you. And vice versa. Neither of you is right or wrong. You are just different. The work of the first year is learning what those differences are and deciding together how to bridge them.

**Stop Trying to Win the Argument**

Here is a hard truth: in a marriage, if you win the argument, you both lose. Every time you fight to be right instead of fighting to be understood, you put a small crack in the foundation. Do enough of that, and the foundation starts to feel unstable.

The shift you need to make is from "winning" to "understanding." That means when a fight breaks out, your goal is not to prove your point. Your goal is to figure out what your partner is actually feeling underneath the words they are using. People rarely fight about what they say they are fighting about. The argument about dishes is usually about feeling unseen. The fight about money is often about feeling controlled or unsafe. The clash over in-laws is sometimes about loyalty and belonging. When you start asking "What is my partner really feeling right now?" instead of "How do I make them see that I am right?" the whole tone of a conversation can shift.

This does not mean you roll over and agree with everything. It means you lead with curiosity before you lead with defensiveness. Ask questions. Listen without preparing your rebuttal. Try to say back what you heard before responding. It feels awkward at first. But couples who practice this consistently fight less and recover faster when they do fight.

**Set Clear Expectations Early**

Most first-year conflict is rooted in unspoken expectations. You assumed your partner would handle the grocery shopping. They assumed you would. Nobody said it out loud. Now it is three weeks in and you are both annoyed, and neither one of you fully understands why. This plays out in dozens of areas: who handles bills, how clean the house should be, how often you spend time with extended family, how much alone time is acceptable, how you talk to each other when you are stressed.

The fix is simple, even if the conversations are a little uncomfortable. Sit down and talk through the things you have been assuming. Not in the middle of a fight, but on a calm day when you both have time. What does each of you expect when it comes to household tasks? How do you each want to handle finances? What do you need when you are upset and how can your partner help? These conversations feel a little clinical at first. But they save you from months of silent resentment and arguments that seem to spring from nowhere.

You will not cover everything in one sitting. That is fine. Think of it as an ongoing conversation that you return to regularly. Expectations also change as life changes. What works in month two may need to be renegotiated by month eight. Keeping the door open for those talks is one of the most practical things you can do for your marriage.

**Learn Each Other's Conflict Style**

Not everyone fights the same way. Some people go loud. They raise their voice, talk fast, and need to get everything out in the moment. Others shut down. They go quiet, need space, and cannot process anything useful until they have had time to think. When a loud processor is married to someone who shuts down, every disagreement can feel like a disaster. The loud person feels like they are being ignored or stonewalled. The quiet person feels ambushed and overwhelmed. Both of them walk away feeling like the other one is the problem.

Neither style is wrong. They are just different. The key is understanding your own style and your partner's, and then building a small set of agreements around how you handle conflict. This could be as simple as agreeing that either person can call a 20-minute break when things get too heated, with the understanding that you will come back and finish the conversation. Or agreeing that you will not bring up grievances when one of you just walked in from a long day at work. These small agreements do not eliminate conflict. But they create enough structure that conflict does not become chaos.

Also worth noting: timing matters enormously. Picking a fight when your partner is hungry, tired, or stressed almost guarantees a bad outcome. You both deserve to have hard conversations when you are in a reasonably calm state. Give each other that courtesy. It changes the outcome more than you would expect.

**Build a Shared Identity Without Losing Yourself**

One of the quieter pressures of the first year is the tension between becoming a unit and staying an individual. You are building a "we." But you are still also a "me." When that balance tips too far in either direction, things start to strain.

Some couples merge completely and then feel suffocated. They stop doing the things they loved separately. They spend every moment together. One or both people start feeling like they have lost something, and that feeling can quietly turn into resentment. Other couples keep too much distance. They are married but still living largely parallel lives, and over time that separateness breeds disconnection.

What works is intentional balance. Keep the things that make you you. Your friendships, your hobbies, your alone time. And at the same time, actively invest in building shared rituals as a couple. A weekly dinner where phones are put away. A show you watch together. A Sunday morning routine. It does not have to be elaborate. It just needs to be consistent and protected. Those shared rituals become the connective tissue of your marriage. They are what you come back to after a rough week. They remind you that underneath the stress and the adjustments and the arguments, you chose this person and they chose you.

**Repair After a Fight, Every Single Time**

How you fight matters. But how you recover from a fight matters even more. Couples who struggle in the long run are not necessarily the ones who argue the most. They are the ones who never fully repair after an argument. They move on without addressing what happened. The tension lingers. It builds on top of the next argument, and the one after that. Eventually even small fights carry the weight of everything that was never resolved.

Repairing after a conflict means more than just stopping the argument. It means going back to your partner and doing a few things deliberately. Acknowledge what you did or said that was unhelpful. Not as a way of taking all the blame, but as a way of showing that you are self-aware. Say something genuine, not just "I'm sorry if you felt that way," which is not really an apology. Try "I know I raised my voice and that was not fair. I was frustrated but I should not have spoken to you like that." Then talk briefly about what you each actually needed in that moment and did not get. And then, genuinely move on. No scorekeeping. No bringing it back up in the next fight.
